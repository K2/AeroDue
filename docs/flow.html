How we make money

Three streams. None of them tax the free stuff.

Contingency
A cut of the non-automatic recoveries — EU261 and escalated claims — where winning takes real work and a fee is fair.
Subscription
A frequent-flyer membership (~$40–90/yr) for travelers who fly often and want everything watched, hands-off.
B2B2C engine
License the recovery engine to travel insurers, OTAs, and card issuers as a retention feature — the long game.
The moat

A weekend builds v1. Years build the one that's right.

Rules engine
Encodes DOT × EU261 distance bands × every card's specific terms — and stays current as regulations shift. The hard, unglamorous, defensible part.
Data flywheel
Every claim teaches us which airlines stall and which arguments win. Recovery rates compound; new entrants start from zero.
Endgame
Become the rails inside the card issuers and booking apps that already hold the itinerary and the payment — not another logo fighting for a keyword.
If you're about to ask…
QIsn't this just AirHelp?
They're EU-first and fight for EU261. We're US-first and built on the money that's never automatic and never filed — card insurance, escalation, EU261 on top. Different geography, different money.
QWon't airlines just auto-refund now?
That's why we don't sell DOT refunds. The DOT made the refund automatic — it did nothing about the compensation and insurance stacked on top, where 100% still goes unclaimed.
QWhy won't Chase or Amex build it?
Frictionless claims raise their payouts — they've kept the benefit clunky for a decade. We're aligned with the traveler. Our endgame is to license it to them as retention, not fight them.
QHow do you make money if refunds are free?
Contingency on the non-automatic recoveries, a frequent-flyer subscription, and licensing the engine B2B2C. We never take a cut of money the law returns for free.
